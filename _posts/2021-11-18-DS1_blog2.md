<h2 id="introduction">Introduction</h2>
<p>This blog covers <a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021999118307125">Physics Informed Neural Networks</a>, which are basically neural networks that are constrained to obey physical constraints. The blog broadly discusses two directions:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.10561">Data-driven solutions of PDEs</a></li>
<li><a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.10566">Data-driven discovery of PDEs</a></li>
</ul>
<p>In particular, it deals with two distinct types of algorithms, namely</p>
<ul>
<li>A new family of data-efficient spatio-temporal function approximators</li>
<li>Arbitrary accurate RK time steppers with potentially unlimited number of stages</li>
</ul>
<p>We know that deep neural networks are powerful function approximators. They can approximate a nonlinear map from a few – potentially very high dimensional – input and output pairs of data. However, the approximation does not take into account any underlying physical constraints imposed by fundamental principles (such as conservation laws) which are<br>
often given in terms of PDEs. PINNs precisely solve this problem by regularizing the loss functions with these constraints. The resulting approximation enjoys both the power of deep neural nets as function approximators and obeying the underlying physical conservation laws.</p>

<h2 id="implementation-details-and-code">Implementation details and Code</h2>
<p>Since most of the examples considered here involve a small number of training data points, the authors choose to exploit quasi-second order optimization methods such as L-BFGS. They do not consider mini-batching for the same reason and consider the full batch on the update. There are no theoretical guarantees on the existence of the minimizer but the authors observe empirically that as long as the PDE is well-posed the optimization algorithm converges to the correct solution.</p>
<p>The original implementation can be accessed at <a href="https://github.com/maziarraissi/PINNs">https://github.com/maziarraissi/PINNs</a> which builds on top of Tensorflow. Corresponding PyTorch and Julia (Flux) implementations can be accessed at <a href="https://github.com/idrl-lab/idrlnet">https://github.com/idrl-lab/idrlnet</a> and <a href="https://neuralpde.sciml.ai/dev/">https://neuralpde.sciml.ai/dev/</a></p>

<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>The major take-away of the paper can be summarized in the following concluding remarks.</p>
<ul>
<li>The authors introduced PINNs, a new class of universal function approximators that are capable of encoding any underlying physical laws that govern a given data-set (described by PDEs)</li>
<li>They also prove a design for data-driven algorithms that can be used for inferring solutions to general nonlinear PDEs, and constructing computationally efficient physics-informed surrogate models.</li>
</ul>
<p>They also rightly point to some lingering questions that still remain unanswered in the original paper:</p>
<ul>
<li>How deep/wide should the neural network be ? How much data is really needed ?</li>
<li>Why does the algorithm converge to unique values for the parameters of the differential operators, i.e., why is the algorithm not suffering from local optima for the parameters of the differential operator?</li>
<li>Does the network suffer from vanishing gradients for deeper architectures and higher order differential operators? Could this be mitigated by using different activation functions?</li>
<li>Can we improve on initializing the network weights or normalizing the data? How about the choices for the loss function choices (MSE, SSE)? What about the robustness of these networks especially when applied to solve chaotic PDEs/ODEs?</li>
</ul>
